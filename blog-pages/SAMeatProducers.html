Media South African Meat Producers Sound the Alarm as Chicken Import Talks with Brazil Stall

June 26, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

Just days after the South African government lifted part of a ban on chicken imports from Brazil, the situation has taken a turn for the worse and meat producers are growing increasingly worried. The Department of Agriculture had announced in mid-June that it would ease the restrictions imposed in May 2025 after an outbreak of bird flu (Highly Pathogenic Avian Influenza, or HPAI) in Brazil.

This raised hopes that the country would soon start receiving mechanically deboned meat (MDM), a key ingredient used to make popular, low-cost protein foods like polony, viennas, Russians, meat pies, and burgers.However, those hopes have quickly faded.According to the South African Meat Processors Association (SAMPA), Brazil and South Africa have reached a deadlock in negotiations over the import health certificate a technical document required to resume the trade. Brazil has reportedly rejected the proposed wording of the certificate, meaning the ban is effectively still in place.

This ongoing stalemate has left meat processors in a tight spot. MDM stockpiles have already been running low for weeks, and many factories are now out of raw material altogether. Some companies have even started laying off workers as production grinds to a halt.“South Africa does not produce MDM in any meaningful amount,” SAMPA explained. “We rely almost entirely on imports and 95% of that has come from Brazil over the past 12 years.”

The shortage couldn't come at a worse time. Alongside the MDM crisis, South Africa is also facing a major hit to beef supply due to a recent foot-and-mouth disease outbreak at the country's biggest feedlot. On top of that, one of the major poultry producers, DayBreak Foods, has collapsed adding more pressure to an already fragile food system. The result? Meat prices are on the rise, and food security is at serious risk especially for lower income households that depend on processed meats for affordable protein.

Stats SA's inflation data for May already showed meat prices starting to climb, and industry experts say it's only going to get worse unless the impasse with Brazil is resolved soon. SAMPA and other companies, including Eskort, have warned the government that the crisis is threatening not just food supplies and prices, but also jobs. The meat processing industry employs more than 125,000 people across the country.

The association is urging both governments to urgently find a solution before the situation worsens.

“Without immediate action, we're looking at more food shortages, higher prices, job losses, and business closures,” SAMPA warned.
