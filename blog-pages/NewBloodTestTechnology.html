Media New Blood Test Technology Offers Breakthrough in Treatment Response

June 20, 2025 ● Tebuho Zongwana

A groundbreaking new technology is changing how doctors monitor the effectiveness of cancer treatments, offering a faster, less invasive way to track solid tumor response through simple blood samples. Researchers from a team at Harvard Medical School and Massachusetts General Hospital have unveiled a liquid biopsy method that can detect molecular changes in real time, providing critical insights into how tumors are responding to therapies without the need for repeated imaging or surgical biopsies.

The technique, known as ctDNA (circulating tumor DNA) profiling, analyzes fragments of DNA shed by tumors into the bloodstream. By examining specific genetic mutations and epigenetic markers, the test can identify whether a patient is responding positively to treatment—often weeks or even months earlier than conventional scans would indicate. This real-time monitoring allows doctors to make faster and more accurate decisions about whether to continue, change, or stop a therapy.

Dr. Alicia Romero, the lead researcher on the study, emphasized the potential impact of the technology: “This approach can transform the way we treat cancer. By understanding tumor dynamics as they happen, we can tailor treatments with unprecedented precision, sparing patients from ineffective therapies and toxic side effects.”

The test has been shown to be effective across several types of solid tumors, including lung, breast, and colorectal cancers. In clinical trials involving over 1,000 patients, the blood-based monitoring system successfully predicted treatment outcomes with over 85% accuracy. Patients whose ctDNA levels dropped significantly after initiating therapy showed longer survival rates and improved recovery compared to those whose levels remained unchanged.

Beyond its diagnostic value, the technology also opens the door for personalized medicine. Oncologists can use the blood test to detect early signs of resistance to a specific drug and pivot to alternative treatments before the cancer has a chance to progress.

The innovation comes at a time when cancer care is rapidly evolving, with a growing emphasis on targeted therapies and real-time data. Experts say this new tool could not only improve outcomes but also reduce costs by avoiding ineffective treatments and unnecessary procedures.

The FDA is currently reviewing the technology for broader clinical approval. If cleared, the blood test could soon become a standard part of cancer management protocols across hospitals worldwide. As the medical community continues to push for earlier, smarter interventions, this advancement marks a significant step toward a future where cancer treatment is more responsive, less invasive, and deeply personalized.
