In California, mechanical engineer Yasaman Ghasempour is helping lead the development of the largest digital camera ever constructed. Built at SLAC National Accelerator Laboratory, this camera is designed for the Vera C. Rubin Observatory in Chile, where it will capture the most detailed images of the night sky ever recorded. Weighing over three tons and holding a staggering 3.2 billion-pixel resolution, the camera will photograph the entire southern sky every few nights over a ten-year period.

This camera is the heart of the Legacy Survey of Space and Time, a scientific project aimed at understanding the structure and evolution of the universe. By observing distant galaxies, exploding stars, and near-Earth objects in incredible detail, researchers hope to unlock secrets about dark energy, dark matter, and how the universe changes over time. Each night, the system will generate about 20 terabytes of data, creating a vast archive of the cosmos.

Ghasempour has been a key figure in making this possible. Born in Iran and trained as an engineer in the United States, she has worked on critical systems that ensure the camera performs with extreme precision. From managing temperature stability to designing vibration-resistant components, her work ensures that the camera operates without even the slightest distortion. Any error could compromise the massive amount of data being collected, so attention to detail has been crucial.

For Ghasempour, the project is about more than building a machine. It's about creating a tool that will expand human knowledge and allow scientists to ask—and maybe answer—fundamental questions about space and time. Her contribution highlights the often-overlooked role of engineers in scientific discovery. While astronomers study the stars, it's the engineers who build the tools that make those discoveries possible.

Now nearing completion, the camera will soon be shipped to Chile for installation. Once it begins operation, it will offer scientists a living, moving picture of the universe—one that will change how we understand our place in it. Thanks to Ghasempour and her team, the future of space science is about to become a lot clearer.
