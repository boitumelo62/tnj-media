Lanseria takes on OR Tambo with Massive Infrastructure Overhaul

Lanseria International Airport, South Africa’s only privately owned and operated airport, is preparing for some big changes. Located in Gauteng, the airport is laying the foundation for a major upgrade aimed at increasing capacity, improving services, and becoming a key player in regional air travel, especially within the Southern African Development Community and the broader sub Saharan region.

Right now, Lanseria is the fourth busiest airport in the country, with about 1.9 million passengers passing through each year. That number is expected to more than double to over four million by 2032 and reach six million by 2037. Long term, they’re aiming for a massive 18 million passengers by 2050. To support this growth, the airport is launching a large infrastructure expansion. This includes adding more terminal space, increasing parking, relocating fuel and technical facilities, and upgrading critical parts of the airport’s structure.

One of the more immediate concerns they’re addressing is the high cost of long-term parking, which has been a regular complaint.

In response, Lanseria is building a three storey parking garage right across from the terminal. It’s costing them R250 million and will add 1,000 parking bays, bringing the total to 4,000. The airport is banking on its ease of access and convenience as major selling points. According to CEO Rampa Rammopo, it’s quick and simple to get from the plane to your car, and the airport avoids the chaos of a large retail space, focusing instead on efficiency.

Further ahead, they’re considering building a second runway, which could cost between R1 billion and R2 billion. Plans are also well underway for a new cargo precinct, aimed at making Lanseria a key logistics hub in the region. This cargo project is being rolled out over ten years, with construction starting in the third quarter of 2025. It’s a response to the lessons learned during the COVID-19 pandemic when passenger travel dropped sharply and the need to diversify became clear. The hope is that it will drive business growth and improve logistics across the region.

Lanseria is also making use of its available land. The airport owns about 90 hectares of undeveloped property and has just signed its first lease agreement with Bionic Aviation, which will be building a cutting-edge hangar and office complex using around 2,000 square metres of space. On top of that, the airport is in the middle of a five year plan to refurbish its hangars, with six already completed. They're also working on digitising systems, enhancing security, and upgrading service delivery to make life easier for operators and increase returns on investment.

One of the other major upgrades in the pipeline is the rehabilitation of Alpha Taxiway, a key section of the airport built more than 20 years ago. This is meant to extend its lifespan, improve how efficiently aircraft move on the ground, and maintain the airport’s high safety standards. Construction for this is expected to start in early 2026 after all the planning and feasibility studies are wrapped up. To prepare for the expected increase in traffic, Lanseria is also expanding its fuel storage. Currently, it holds about 1,000 cubic metres of fuel, but that will grow to 6,000 cubic metres. This increase should help prevent supply issues and make sure airlines can count on the airport to meet their needs.

With all these developments, Rammopo says Lanseria isn’t just trying to offer an alternative to OR Tambo International Airport — it wants to compete directly and be seen as a major player in South African and regional aviation.
