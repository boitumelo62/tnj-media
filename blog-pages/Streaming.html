What Happens When Millions of South Africans Lose Access to Free-to-Air TV?

As global streaming growth slows, Africa is stepping into the spotlight. With its young, mobile- first population and growing demand for digital content, the continent is becoming a key focus for platforms looking to scale.

In South Africa alone, over 8.4 million people now use paid or subscription-based streaming services. And the market is still growing fast, with projections pointing to an 8% annual growth rate through 2028

But what works elsewhere won't necessarily work here. Africa isn't one market, it's many each with its own realities. In places like South Africa, users jump between mobile data and Wi-Fi, want flexible pricing, and expect content in their own languages. To succeed, platforms must build from the ground up with local habits, culture and constraints in mind.

Cost is a major factor. Many users are price-sensitive and practical they want value, not just entertainment. That's why hybrid models that mix free, ad-supported content with paid upgrades are gaining traction. Short-term or low-cost options like daily subscriptions are key

Data costs also matter. Telco partnerships that offer data-inclusive bundles or zero rated content make a big difference. At Viu, for example, working with mobile networks has helped lower the barriers for many South Africans.

Free, ad-supported streaming isn't just a stop gap , it's a smart and scalable way to grow. It brings in new users and gives advertisers access to a mobile-first, highly engaged audience. But ads need to reflect local languages, cultural cues and values to really land.

These aren't passive consumers. They're young, brand-aware and intentional. Platforms and advertisers that show up authentically will win their attention and loyalty.

Africa's audience is ready. Infrastructure is improving, smartphones are everywhere, and cultural pride is rising. But the content must reflect real African lives in local languages, across genres, and made by local creators.

This is more than entertainment. It's about economic opportunity, creative ownership and cultural influence.

Africa isn't playing catch-up. It's building its own streaming future, mobile first grounded in local relevance, and shaped by how people actually live
