Business South African Companies Pushing Return to Office Policies Face Legal Risks

June 17, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

More and more businesses in South Africa are ending their work-from-home (WFH) policies and requiring employees to return to the office. But legal experts are warning companies to be careful because pushing a return to office (RTO) order without following the right process could land them in legal trouble.

The Labour Court has already made it clear, employers cannot simply force employees back to the office without proper consultation. And they definitely can't use RTO orders as a reason to punish or fire workers who don't comply especially if those employees had valid remote work agreements in place.

According to legal experts from the law firm Bowmans, recent Labour Court rulings have reinforced that RTO instructions must be reasonable and employers should engage with staff before making any changes. This means giving employees a chance to understand the company's reasoning and to raise their concerns. That kind of process not only builds trust but also helps avoid claims of unfair treatment later on.

One recent case in May 2025 illustrates the risks. An employee who had been working from home during the Covid-19 pandemic due to health reasons was instructed to return to the office once the situation began to improve. When she didn't comply, the company launched a disciplinary process and dismissed her for gross misconduct.

However, the employee challenged the dismissal, and both the Labour Court and the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) found that her firing was unfair both in terms of the reasons given and the process followed.

This case shows that employers can't simply reverse WFH agreements at will. Once a remote work arrangement has been negotiated, it becomes part of the working conditions and changing it requires a fair process. If not handled properly, enforcing a return to the office could expose companies to legal challenges and reputational damage.
