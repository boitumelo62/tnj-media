Media Multichoice Swings Back to Profit with Big Cost Cuts

June 13, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

Multichoice, the company behind DStv, has made a strong financial comeback. After posting heavy losses of R2.9 billion and R4 billion in 2023 and 2024, the company reported a profit of R1.8 billion for the financial year ending 31 March 2025.

This turnaround was helped by several factors: major cost cutting efforts, a stronger rand, and a deal to sell 60% of its insurance business (NMSIS) to Sanlam, which brought in R1.2 billion after tax. A large portion of the profit came from what the company claims is R3.7 billion in cost savings nearly double its original target of R2 billion

However, when BusinessTech reviewed the financials, only about R1.5 billion in cost savings could be directly seen in the income statement. That included R300 million saved in service and product costs and R1.2 billion from reduced admin and selling expenses. The remaining R2.2 billion in savings raised eyebrows.

Multichoice CFO Tim Jacobs explained that the R3.7 billion figure includes "realised" savings costs the company actively managed or negotiated down (like content deals or marketing) rather than just reductions seen in the income statement. He noted that 42% of these savings came from content-related spending alone, with other areas including renegotiated contracts, sales and marketing cuts, and reduced technology and transmission costs.

Despite the debate over the exact savings figure, it's clear that Multichoice is cutting back significantly and getting its finances under control. It's also no longer technically insolvent, meaning its assets now outweigh its liabilities. The company improved its financial position by reducing lease liabilities, loans, and taxes and even repaid R927 million early on a R12 billion loan.

Still, the company's total assets dropped by 10.5%, largely due to lower spending on content and reduced cash reserves. At the end of the financial year, it held R5.1 billion in cash, with another R3 billion available through credit if needed

Overall, Multichoice appears to be tightening its belt and moving in a more stable direction, even as questions remain around the full picture of its cost savings.
