Sinaloa Cartel Exploited Hacked Surveillance to Target FBI Informants

A newly released U.S. Department of Justice audit has revealed that the Sinaloa drug cartel used a hacker in 2018 to infiltrate the mobile phone of an FBI assistant legal attaché based at the U.S. Embassy in Mexico City. The breach provided the cartel with sensitive call logs and geolocation data, which they combined with access to public surveillance cameras to track the agent and identify informants.

Using the attaché's phone number, the hacker was able to collect detailed information about calls and movements. This intelligence, together with access to Mexico City's CCTV network, enabled the cartel to monitor meetings and movements, helping them locate and, in some cases, eliminate individuals cooperating with U.S. authorities.

The cartel involved was the notorious Sinaloa organization, previously led by Joaquín “El Chapo” Guzmán, who has been serving a life sentence in the United States since 2017.

The Justice Department's inspector general warned that the incident highlights the increasing threat posed by criminal groups using digital surveillance tools. The audit emphasized that such capabilities are no longer limited to nation-states and can be exploited by non-state actors, including organized crime syndicates.

In response, the report recommended that the FBI implement a comprehensive threat assessment strategy, improve security protocols, and enhance training to prevent similar breaches in the future.

This alarming breach demonstrates how emerging technologies, if left unsecured, can be turned into powerful weapons against even the most well-resourced law enforcement agencies.
