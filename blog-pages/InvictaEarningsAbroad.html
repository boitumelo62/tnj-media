Business Earnings Without Borders

July 4, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

Invicta Holdings, led by billionaire Christo Wiese, is planning to generate at least half of its income from outside South Africa within the next two years. The company outlined this strategic goal in its financial results for the year ending March 2025, pointing to a tough operating environment at home.

Challenges such as unreliable electricity, inadequate infrastructure, regulatory delays, and ongoing political and economic uncertainty have pushed Invicta to diversify its income sources geographically.

Wiese, known for building Shoprite and Pep into household names, said that geographic diversification and sustainable earnings are central to Invicta's long-term strategy. He emphasized that the company's core business — supplying parts, components, and consumables — remains essential to economic activity, regardless of market conditions.

Invicta has made several moves to expand its global presence. In the United States, the company launched a new start-up, KSP, through its joint venture with Kian Ann Engineering. This new operation aims to grow Invicta's distribution of undercarriage machinery and complements its existing American business, KTSU. In the United Kingdom, Invicta acquired Nationwide Bearing Company to further strengthen its European footprint.

As part of its broader restructuring, Invicta sold its entire stake in KMP Holdings to its joint venture partner Kian Ann, and relocated a Singapore-based distribution center to China. Wiese said the sale aligned offshore operations while allowing Invicta to retain an interest in KMP's future performance.

The deal resulted in a once-off profit of R222 million, which contributed significantly to a 57% jump in basic earnings per share from 492 cents to 773 cents. Revenue increased by 6% to R8.11 billion, while operating profit before foreign exchange movements rose 21% to R765.43 million. Net asset value per share also grew by 13% to 5,931 cents.

Over the past year, the company redeemed nearly 7 million preference shares and repurchased 4.9 million ordinary shares as part of its efforts to improve shareholder value. While Invicta continues to expand globally, it remains alert to domestic risks.

The company noted that the formation of South Africa's Government of National Unity had lifted market confidence, resulting in stronger performance in the latter part of the year. However, it warned that long-term structural challenges remain, including political instability, labour unrest, and the deindustrialisation of the South African economy.

One particular concern is the closure of major steelmaking operations in 2025, which the company believes could severely impact the country's industrial base.

Invicta also cautioned that government efforts to protect local industries could backfire by increasing inefficiencies and weakening competitiveness.

Despite these headwinds, Wiese remains confident in the group's direction. He credited the company's solid performance to operational discipline and the ability to adapt strategically, even in difficult markets.
