Media South Africa's TV Planning Landscape Set for Transformation

June 13, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

Over the next two years, South Africa's TV planning landscape is set for two major shifts one in 2026 and a much more transformative one in 2027.

Starting in the first quarter of 2026, the new TAMS (Television Audience Measurement Survey) universe will go live. This change is based on a new Establishment Survey (ES) being conducted during 2025. While this update might seem relatively minor, it's still significant because the current panel setup relies on outdated data PAMS 2019 for the panel and 2020 figures for pay-TV subscribers. Since then, the TV environment in South Africa has changed considerably, with how people access and consume TV content becoming much more complex.

Ask Africa has been appointed to run this crucial research, and once complete, it will give us a more accurate picture of the current TV landscape. However, we won't fully grasp the impact of these changes until the new universe data is used in planning.

This transition won't be seamless. There will be a period where old and new data sets overlap where planning might be based on outdated figures, while performance results reflect the updated data. During this time, marketers, agencies, and broadcasters will need to be flexible with deliverables, cost-per-point deals, and performance targets.

Then, in 2027, an even more dramatic change is coming: the introduction of Total Video Measurement (TVM). This will redefine how TV and video audiences are measured in South Africa. The idea is to create a single system that captures audience behaviour across every type of video platform live TV, catch-up viewing (BVOD), streaming services (OTT), social media, mobile, and even out-of-home viewing.

TVM isn't just about collecting more data. It's about creating a trusted, standardised framework that broadcasters, advertisers, and agencies can use for planning, trading, and evaluating content. It's being designed to meet international standards while being flexible enough to deal with uniquely South African challenges like electricity blackouts, uneven internet access, and a mix of urban and rural viewing habits.

Once this new system is up and running, software providers like Arianna and Transmit will also need to evolve. These tools were designed for the existing local market, and they'll need significant updates to handle the complexity of TVM data. In fact, international planning tools often look and function very differently, reflecting the more trade-focused nature of global markets.

To help everyone adjust, the plan is to run both the old and new systems in parallel for a while. This should give broadcasters, agencies, and marketers time to learn, compare, and make sense of the changes before any new performance benchmarks are set in stone.

It's understandable that this all might feel overwhelming. But these changes are both necessary and overdue. South Africa's media environment is evolving rapidly, and these steps will finally bring measurement and planning up to speed with how people actually watch video today across all screens, all platforms, and all times of day.
