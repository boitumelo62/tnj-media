Media Africa's PR Industry Embraces Change While Holding Firm on Ethics

June 17, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

Last week, the African Public Relations Association (APRA), which is celebrating its 50th anniversary this year, teamed up with the Public Relations and Communications Association (PRCA) to share the latest findings from their annual research into ethics and the state of public relations across Africa. The report was presented during APRA's annual conference in Mombasa, Kenya, and was conducted by Reputation Matters, a leading African research consultancy

This study has now been running for six years, tracking ethical standards and, for the past four years, looking at broader trends in the PR industry. For the second year in a row, the research also examined the growing influence of artificial intelligence (AI) on the profession. This year's edition was the most inclusive yet, with 313 PR professionals from 24 African countries taking part. South Africa had the largest share of responses, followed by Kenya, Nigeria, Cameroon, and Mauritius. The responses paint a diverse picture of a profession navigating change while holding firmly to ethical foundations.

The findings show a communications industry under pressure from fast-moving digital innovation, but still rooted in key values. Corruption remains the most pressing ethical challenge, with many respondents pointing to broader issues like cultural norms, social injustice, and weak law enforcement as factors that erode public trust. At the same time, the study found that 69% of participants view AI as a valuable tool that can support, rather than replace, traditional PR functions. Still, concerns remain over how this technology could be used to spread misinformation or manipulate audiences.

There are signs of progress when it comes to PR's role in the boardroom. While 40% of respondents say the profession is still undervalued at leadership level, 38% note an increase in how often business leaders rely on PR advice a notable rise from last year's 31%. However, many professionals still feel there are gaps in training, particularly in areas like crisis communication, strategic planning, and reputation management.

Despite a slight drop in the overall number of survey responses compared to 2024, the broader regional participation suggests that PR is maturing across the continent. There is a growing recognition of its value in building trust, especially in times of institutional or reputational crisis.

APRA President Arik Karani emphasized the importance of ethical communication in today's uncertain world, saying, “In an era where trust is fragile, the value of ethical public relations has never been clearer.” Sarah Waddington CBE, interim CEO of PRCA, echoed this, noting that while the profession is showing more awareness of its responsibilities, it must continue to strengthen its commitment to ethics and responsible AI use.

The report identifies three key pillars for the future of PR in Africa: ethical clarity, strategic credibility, and ongoing professional growth. Regine le Roux, managing director of Reputation Matters, summed it up by saying that PR today is about more than just managing public perception it's about building trust and driving positive change.

To continue the conversation, PRCA Africa and APRA will host a live panel discussion through the Ethics and Practices Network. The session will explore the report's insights further and focus on how ethics can guide the profession in a rapidly changing digital world.
