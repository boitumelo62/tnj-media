Media South Africa Eases Brazil Chicken Ban to Avoid Food Crisis

The Department of Agriculture has announced a partial lifting of the ban on poultry imports from Brazil, a move aimed at easing pressure on South Africa's food security

The country initially banned Brazilian poultry imports in May after an avian flu outbreak in the Rio Grande do Sul region. The ban sent ripples through the local meat industry, especially affecting the supply of mechanically deboned meat (MDM) a key ingredient used to make affordable meat products like polony, viennas, Russians, and braaiwors.

South Africa doesn't produce MDM locally and imports roughly 19,000 tonnes of it from Brazil every month. Without it, major producers like Eskort warned that low cost meat options could disappear from shelves, leaving many households without affordable protein options.

The timing couldn't have been worse. Alongside the chicken import ban, South Africa was also dealing with a foot-and-mouth disease outbreak at the country's biggest beef feedlot and the collapse of poultry producer DayBreak Foods. These combined challenges threatened to drive meat prices even higher.

Meat processors and importers urgently called on the government to lift the ban, highlighting that the country was losing over 100 million meals per week. Responding to these concerns, the department has now agreed to allow imports from other regions of Brazil not affected by the flu, starting 19 June.

Industry players have welcomed the decision, but they're also warning that damage has already been done. The four-week ban disrupted supply chains, forced some processors to halt operations, and raised fears about job losses and wider social consequences

Eskort pointed out that even a month-long break in MDM supply could leave many processors inactive for more than 60 days. SAMPA (the South African Meat Processors Association) echoed this, saying the partial lifting of the ban will help avoid major shortages and protect jobs but stressed that authorities must now be better prepared in case other parts of Brazil face similar outbreaks.

While the partial ban lift comes as a relief, it highlights how dependent South Africa is on international supply chains for affordable meat, and just how quickly disruptions can become national issues.
