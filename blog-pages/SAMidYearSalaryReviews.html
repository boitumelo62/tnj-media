Business What South Africans Should Know About Mid Year Salary Reviews

June 27, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

For many South Africans working in both the public and private sectors, the middle of the year brings a mix of anticipation and hope. It's the time when mid year salary reviews take place, and with them, the possibility of a much needed raise. After months of dedication and hard work, employees naturally look forward to some financial relief.

However, salary increases don't happen without an influence . They are deeply tied to the broader economic environment, which remains unpredictable and complex.Lindiwe Sebesho, a Master Reward Specialist and Executive Committee Member of the South African Reward Association, explains that while inflation has improved in recent months with consumer price inflation averaging around 3% as of April 2025 there are still many challenges at play, both locally and globally, that affect what companies can afford.

She encourages employees to approach salary discussions with an understanding of these economic realities. Doing so can lead to more constructive conversations and healthier relationships between employers and employees, while also supporting greater productivity across the country.There are several key factors that influence an employer's ability to offer salary increases. These include the overall performance of the economy, how well individual companies are doing, trends in the job market, and whether certain skills are in high demand

Even though inflation is currently stable and within the South African Reserve Bank's target range of 3-6%, interest rates remain high, putting strain on both employees and employers. Other pressures include rising fuel and food costs, which continue to affect budgets despite some relief from potential lower fuel prices. The recent increase in the fuel levy, for example, adds to transport and logistics costs across sectors.

Climate related challenges like drought are also pushing up food prices, further tightening household spending.On top of local issues, South Africa is also feeling the effects of weaker GDP growth than expected and global economic uncertainty. For example, fluctuating trade policies in the United States, including tariffs affecting South Africa's trading partners, could contribute to local price increases. This kind of instability makes businesses cautious about increasing salaries, even when they want to support their staff.

Sebesho emphasizes the need for a balanced approach to salary increases. She points out that it's not about undervaluing employees, but rather about ensuring that any changes to compensation are sustainable for the business and support long term job security. In other words, salary negotiations need to reflect not just personal needs, but also the broader financial health of the organisation.While the economic landscape might feel discouraging, there are still ways employees can improve their chances during salary review time. Being informed about your market value is a good starting point. Use reliable sources to check whether your pay is in line with industry standards, and consider your employer's own pay structures and policies. It also helps to be empathetic.

If you understand your employer's financial pressures, you're more likely to have a constructive discussion that leads to a fair outcome. Demonstrating your value is another crucial step. Employers are more inclined to reward strong performance, so be ready to show how your work has made a meaningful impact on your team or the business.It's also worth remembering that your overall compensation includes more than just your monthly paycheck. Consider the full package things like medical aid, retirement contributions, paid time off, and other perks.

In some cases, adjusting or optimising these benefits could help relieve financial pressure without requiring a direct salary increase.When it's time to have that conversation, it's natural to feel nervous or even frustrated especially if you were counting on a raise. But it's important to remain realistic and respectful. Many employers are under pressure themselves and may not have the flexibility to meet every request.

Being understanding without selling yourself short can go a long way. Sebesho concludes that while employees have every right to expect fair and equitable pay, keeping both sides of the conversation in mind is essential. A balanced discussion not only improves the chances of a successful outcome but also helps protect long term employment in a tough economic climate.
