Study Links HIV Medication to Accelerated Knee Osteoarthritis

A new study has raised concerns about a class of HIV medications potentially accelerating the development of knee osteoarthritis (OA) in people living with the virus.

Published on June 3 in Bone Research, the study was led by Dr. Zanjing Zhai of Shanghai Jiaotong University and explored the effects of protease inhibitors (PIs)—specifically lopinavir and ritonavir—on joint health. While antiretroviral therapies have transformed HIV from a fatal disease to a manageable condition, they may come with unintended long-term effects.

Researchers analyzed data from 151 HIV-infected individuals and found those treated with PIs scored lower on the Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score, suggesting worse joint function. X-rays also revealed a higher incidence of OA in the knees of PI-treated patients.

The team then examined how these drugs affect chondrocytes—the cells that form cartilage, which deteriorates in OA. Of 25 HIV drugs tested, lopinavir showed the most harmful effects, accelerating cell degradation and triggering senescence, a process where cells permanently stop dividing.

Digging deeper, researchers identified the gene Zmpste24 as a key player. Lopinavir was found to suppress this gene, destabilizing the nuclear membrane and activating the p53 pathway—known to speed up cartilage aging. However, when Zmpste24 was absent in lab models, the damaging effects of lopinavir disappeared. Enhancing the gene's expression also reduced OA severity in mice.

“This study provides new insight into how HIV treatment regimens may contribute to early-onset osteoarthritis,” said Dr. Zhai. “Patients at higher risk for joint degeneration should consider alternative therapies when possible.”

The findings may influence future treatment strategies for millions living with HIV, particularly those already vulnerable to musculoskeletal conditions.
