Politics South Africa's Government of National Unity Off to a Cautious but Promising Start

June 24, 2025 ● Tebuho Zongwana

South Africa's newly formed Government of National Unity (GNU) is showing cautious but promising progress as it settles into power following the country's most closely contested general election since 1994. The election, which left no single party with a majority, led to a historic coalition between the African National Congress (ANC), Democratic Alliance (DA), Inkatha Freedom Party (IFP), and several smaller parties.

President Cyril Ramaphosa, who retained his position as head of state, has called the GNU a “new chapter of inclusive governance” aimed at rebuilding public trust, stabilizing the economy, and delivering basic services more effectively. The Cabinet, formed earlier this month, reflects the coalition's shared governance, with key ministries divided between parties. The DA now leads portfolios such as public service and trade, while the IFP has been assigned traditional affairs and rural development.

Parliament has resumed under Speaker Annelie Lotriet (DA), elected through a joint agreement among GNU partners. Committees are working to align on policy goals for the second half of 2025, with an emphasis on improving infrastructure, reducing corruption, and boosting investor confidence.

Economically, Finance Minister Enoch Godongwana unveiled a revised budget policy focused on fiscal responsibility, green energy development, and support for small businesses. The rand has shown moderate stability, and credit rating agencies have maintained South Africa's outlook as neutral, citing the potential of a functioning multi-party government.

Public response has been mixed. Some South Africans are hopeful that shared power could bring greater accountability, while others remain skeptical about how the coalition will handle long-standing challenges like load-shedding, unemployment, and crime. “I'm tired of promises. We need real change, not more talk,” said Soweto resident Mpho Radebe.

Policy friction has already surfaced over issues like land reform, economic empowerment, and foreign relations, particularly regarding Israel and the Russia-Ukraine conflict. Analysts warn that ideological divisions could threaten unity if not carefully managed.

Still, political experts say the GNU represents a significant moment in South African democracy. “It's an opportunity to reset the country's direction through compromise and shared accountability,” said political analyst Lebo Makhubela. “But it will only work if the focus stays on delivery, not political survival.”

The GNU has committed to showing tangible results within its first 100 days—a promise many are watching closely. Whether this experiment in coalition politics delivers lasting change remains uncertain, but for now, the country stands at a critical turning point.
