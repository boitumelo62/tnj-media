Media Iran Imposes Nationwide Internet Shutdown Amid Escalating Regional Tensions

Tehran, 19 June 2025 — The Iranian government has implemented a widespread internet blackout across the country, significantly limiting access to global websites and online services. The move comes amid rising tensions with Israel and concerns over cyberattacks targeting Iranian infrastructure.

The shutdown began on 13 June and has since escalated into one of the most severe internet restrictions the country has imposed in recent years. By 18 June, connectivity had dropped to less than 5% of normal traffic, according to international internet monitoring agencies. While Iran's domestic intranet system—the National Information Network (NIN)—remains operational, access to international platforms such as WhatsApp, Instagram, Google, and app stores has been effectively blocked.

Government officials claim the restrictions are a temporary national security measure aimed at preventing foreign interference and cyber threats, particularly from Israel. However, rights groups and digital watchdogs argue that the blackout serves a dual purpose: shielding the government from international scrutiny and suppressing domestic dissent.

The shutdown has disrupted critical sectors, including banking, commerce, and communications. Major banks have reported outages, and popular cryptocurrency exchanges such as Nobitex have faced connectivity issues. Citizens have also struggled to access emergency information and news from outside the country.

In an attempt to bypass restrictions, SpaceX's Starlink satellite internet service has reportedly been activated over Iran, offering limited access to uncensored information for journalists, activists, and select users. The Iranian government has responded by warning against unauthorized internet access and urging the public to delete WhatsApp, alleging it may be compromised by foreign intelligence—claims denied by the platform's parent company, Meta.

Iran has a history of cutting internet access during periods of unrest, including during mass protests in 2019 and 2022. This latest blackout, however, is broader in scope and linked directly to escalating geopolitical conflict, rather than internal demonstrations.

As of now, the government has provided no clear timeline for the restoration of full internet access. Analysts warn the shutdown could last several more days or weeks, deepening Iran's digital isolation at a time of heightened regional instability.
