Toxic Trends in Online Dating: Ghosting and Breadcrumbing Take Emotional Toll

A recent psychological study has shed light on the emotional harm caused by two increasingly common online dating behaviors—ghosting and breadcrumbing—revealing their deep and lasting impact on mental well-being.

Ghosting, the act of abruptly cutting off communication without explanation, has become widespread across dating apps. Around 20% of users report being ghosted, with many describing feelings of confusion, self-doubt, and emotional abandonment. The sudden silence often leaves individuals without closure, exacerbating feelings of rejection and anxiety.

Breadcrumbing, a more subtle form of emotional manipulation, involves sporadic and insincere communication to maintain someone's interest without real intent to pursue a relationship. Approximately 35% of users admit to having breadcrumbed or being breadcrumbed. Experts warn that this behavior traps victims in cycles of hope and disappointment, leading to reduced self-esteem and increased loneliness.

The study also found that misrepresentation in dating profiles contributes to trust issues. On average, users include about 7% false information in their profiles, often to appear more attractive. This digital dishonesty can damage the foundation of potential relationships before they begin.

Psychologists advocate for greater transparency and empathy in online interactions. Notably, two-thirds of young adult relationships begin as friendships, suggesting that genuine human connection remains key to meaningful partnerships.

As dating apps continue to dominate the search for love, researchers urge users to communicate honestly, avoid manipulative behaviors, and remember the real people behind the profiles.
