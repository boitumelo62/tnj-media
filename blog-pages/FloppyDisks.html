Tech News Floppy Disks Still in Use in 2025—Here's Why

June 17, 2025 ● Tebuho Zongwana

In an age dominated by terabyte cloud storage, lightning-fast SSDs, and AI-generated data, it might come as a surprise that some systems around the world are still powered—at least in part—by 3.5-inch floppy disks.

Yes, the same floppy disks that many thought died out with dial-up internet and CRT monitors are still being used in critical industries today. While modern tech continues to evolve at a breakneck pace, these legacy storage devices continue to quietly fulfil roles no other technology has quite managed to replace—yet.

In the aviation world, floppy disks are more than just nostalgia. Certain older aircraft, including some models of Boeing 747s and early Airbus planes, still use floppy disks to load navigation data and update software. These aircraft, many of which are still in operation in smaller airlines or cargo fleets, rely on legacy systems that were originally designed in the 1980s and 1990s.

On factory floors, decades-old machines still operate using embedded systems that require floppy disks for data input. In some hospitals and labs, older diagnostic equipment also continues to rely on floppies, especially in regions where budgets are tight.

Some government and military systems use floppy disks as a form of air-gapped security. Without internet connectivity, these low-capacity disks offer a surprising level of protection from cyberattacks.

Beyond industry, floppy disks have found new life among digital artists and retro tech fans. Albums, games, and pixel art stored on floppy disks are part of a growing niche movement celebrating digital minimalism.

Though no longer mass-produced, floppy disks are still available through specialty vendors and online sellers. A handful of companies even manufacture new disks to meet ongoing demand.

Despite their age, floppy disks are still quietly spinning in aircraft, factories, and creative spaces—proof that sometimes old tech refuses to die.
