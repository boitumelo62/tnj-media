Media Scientists Use mRNA to Force HIV Out of Hiding — A Major Step Toward a Cure

HIV has remained incurable for more than four decades largely because of its ability to hide. Even when people take antiretroviral therapy (ART) that suppresses the virus to undetectable levels, HIV doesn't go away. Instead, it retreats into so-called “latent reservoirs” — pockets of infected immune cells where the virus lies dormant and invisible to both the immune system and HIV drugs.

But now, researchers at the University of California, San Francisco (UCSF), in partnership with the National Institutes of Health (NIH), have found a new way to force the virus out of hiding using messenger RNA — or mRNA.

mRNA, short for messenger Ribonucleic acid, is a type of molecule that tells cells what proteins to make. Think of it as a set of temporary instructions — a biological text message — that the cell reads and uses to build specific proteins.

This technology became widely known during the COVID-19 pandemic, when mRNA vaccines taught cells how to make a harmless piece of the coronavirus spike protein, training the immune system to recognize and fight the real virus. Now, scientists are using the same idea to tackle HIV in a completely different way.

In this study, the scientists used specially designed mRNA to deliver instructions into immune cells, telling them to produce a protein that reactivates dormant HIV. Once the virus is active again, it becomes visible to the immune system and can potentially be targeted and destroyed — either by the body's natural defenses or with additional treatment.

This is part of a broader approach researchers call “shock and kill.” The goal is to “shock” the hidden virus into revealing itself, then “kill” it.

Previous efforts to do this often caused unwanted side effects or only partially woke the virus. But the mRNA method appears both effective and safe. In lab tests using blood from people living with HIV who are on ART, the mRNA technique successfully reactivated the virus in over 80% of samples — without triggering harmful inflammation or damaging healthy cells.

“We've shown that mRNA can wake up HIV in a controlled, precise way,” said Dr. Anjali Deshmukh, the study's lead researcher. “That's a major step toward clearing the virus completely from the body.”

While this doesn't cure HIV on its own, it's a critical step in the right direction. The reactivated virus still needs to be eliminated. To do that, scientists are now working on pairing this mRNA-based "shock" with immune therapies or vaccines that could deliver the "kill."

The findings are still in early stages, but they represent one of the most promising uses of mRNA in HIV cure research to date. Clinical trials in people could begin in the next couple of years.

“This is a powerful example of how the lessons from COVID-19 are now helping us fight one of the world's toughest viruses,” said Dr. Carl Dieffenbach, director of the Division of AIDS at the NIH. “We're not there yet, but this brings us closer than ever to a functional cure.”
