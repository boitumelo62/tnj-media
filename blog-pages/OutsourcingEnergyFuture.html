Business Outsourcing the Energy Future

June 30, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

Progress with Purpose: The Employment Equity Act's Built-In Flexibility for Employers

South Africa is trying to shift away from coal and move toward cleaner, fairer energy solutions a process known as the Just Energy Transition (JET). The idea is to cut carbon emissions while also protecting the rights and livelihoods of workers and communities affected by this shift. But a new investigation is raising serious concerns about where the money meant to fund this transition is actually going.

On 26 June 2025, a civil society group called Open Secrets released a report titled The Climate Consultants. It shows that a large chunk of the international funding South Africa has received for its energy transition isn't being used to support local efforts, but is instead flowing into the pockets of global consulting firms.

Many of these firms are based in the very countries that are donating the money countries that are publicly supporting South Africa's transition but, behind the scenes, benefiting financially from it. South Africa's own estimates say that the transition will cost around $100 billion (or R1.76 trillion). So far, international partners have pledged roughly $12.8 billion (R225.69 billion), but only a small portion about 6% comes in the form of grants. That means most of the money is either loans or investments with strings attached.

The report reveals that nearly two-thirds of the grant money has been directed toward private companies and foreign institutions not to local non-profits, universities, or public agencies that are closer to the ground and more likely to understand local needs. This is deeply concerning to the people behind the report, especially as South Africa becomes the first Global South country to formally partner with international donors on such a large scale for climate transition.

Michael Marchant from Open Secrets, who co-authored the report, warned that consultants now play a powerful role in shaping national energy policies. But these firms aren't acting out of public interest. They're businesses, driven by profit. He argued that relying so heavily on outside consultants especially ones who aren't transparent about their work threatens democracy. It reduces the state's ability to build its own expertise and makes it easier for corporations, some with links to the fossil fuel industry, to influence public policy.

The report also points to clear conflicts of interest. Some of these firms advise both the government and big fossil fuel companies. Despite this, they've been hired to help steer South Africa's response to climate change and the energy crisis. Their growing power is often unchecked and rarely questioned, even though some of them have been linked to past corruption and scandals, both in South Africa and abroad.

Zen Mathe, another investigator at Open Secrets, highlighted that this is especially troubling in a country still grappling with the fallout of state capture. She said that allowing these firms to dominate such an important space without strict oversight is dangerous especially since they haven't always been held accountable for their actions in the past.

Open Secrets believes the public deserves to know more about how these consultants are influencing critical decisions around energy, climate, and economic reform. The group is calling for greater transparency and accountability. They say it's time to question whether these firms should have so much control over policies that affect everyone, and to ensure that South Africa's energy transition remains fair, inclusive, and truly serves the public good.
