Media KZN Flood Fallout: Toyota Insurer Takes Government to Court

Toyota's insurance company, Tokio Marine & Nichido Fire Insurance, is suing the KwaZulu- Natal provincial government, Transnet, and the eThekwini Municipality for R6.5 billion in damages.

This legal action stems from the extensive damage suffered by Toyota South Africa Motors' Prospecton plant in Durban during the April 2022 floods. The insurer claims that the responsible authorities failed to maintain crucial infrastructure designed to protect the industrial area from flooding, which led directly to the disaster.

According to court papers filed in the Durban High Court, the R6.5 billion claim includes R4.5 billion for repair and reinstatement work and R2 billion for business interruption losses. While Toyota South Africa Motors is listed as the official plaintiff, Tokio Marine is the one actively pursuing the lawsuit. This is possible because of a legal principle called subrogation, which allows an insurer to recover losses from third parties responsible for the damage, effectively stepping into the insured's shoes.

Toyota clarified that it is not funding or managing the legal proceedings and will not benefit financially from the case. The lawsuit names Transnet, the KZN Department of Transport, and the eThekwini Municipality as defendants. The insurer alleges that these entities failed to maintain vital flood prevention systems, including the Umlaas Canal and an adjacent diversion berm, both of which are critical in managing stormwater and protecting the Prospecton Industrial Area.

Transnet is said to own and be responsible for maintaining the Umlaas Canal, a concrete- lined channel designed to divert the uMlazi River. The Department of Transport is accused of neglecting the maintenance of the diversion berm, while the eThekwini Municipality was responsible for the broader stormwater network. The insurer argues that the defendants' collective failure to repair earlier damage, perform regular maintenance, and respond effectively to risks resulted in stormwater breaching the berm and flooding the area.

The flooding caused severe structural damage to the plant and its systems, including electrical installations, plumbing, air conditioning, and assembly lines. Additionally, thousands of vehicles were damaged beyond repair, along with equipment and office contents. Operations at the plant were halted for more than three months, resulting in significant production losses.

In response to the devastation, Toyota launched an intensive recovery effort, supported by its global team. The plant resumed production in August 2022. Despite the quick turnaround, the extended shutdown led to the loss of thousands of vehicles that could not be built during that period.

Since then, Toyota South Africa Motors has invested around R236 million to protect the facility from future flooding. This includes R108 million spent on monitoring and maintenance systems, such as early warning weather alerts and improved stormwater canals. An additional R128 million was used for internal upgrades like raising doorways, installing bund walls around key infrastructure, and making other changes to protect the plant even if external systems fail.

The 2022 floods were declared a national disaster by President Cyril Ramaphosa and caused widespread damage across KwaZulu-Natal. Toyota's Prospecton plant was among the hardest hit industrial sites, with experts describing the event as the most significant damage ever recorded at any Toyota production facility worldwide.
