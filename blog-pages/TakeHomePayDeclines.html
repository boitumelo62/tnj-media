Business Take Home Pay Declines for Third Month as Economic Pressures Mount

June 25, 2025 ● Uhone Kwinda

The latest figures from BankservAfrica show that the average take home pay in South Africa has declined for the third month in a row, with May 2025's average slipping to R17,296.

That's about 1.3% lower than in April, when the average stood at R17,532. Still, compared to May 2024, the figure is higher then, the average take-home pay was R15,903. This ongoing slowdown reflects a struggling economic environment. The country saw little to no growth in the first quarter of 2025, and global uncertainty especially the instability in the Middle East is weighing heavily on the outlook.

BankservAfrica, which compiles its data from about 3.8 million South African salary earners, noted that this downward trend is being felt in both nominal and real terms. Once adjusted for inflation, real take home pay dropped to R14,832 in May, down from R15,003 in April. Even so, earnings in real terms remain nearly 6% higher than a year ago, helped by inflation that has eased significantly headline inflation for May was recorded at just 2.8%.

Although prices eased again after ceasefire talks, the risk of further volatility remains high.South Africa's fuel prices are already under pressure. Daily data suggests an under- recovery of between 50 and 80 cents per litre, which could translate into a hike of around R1.00 per litre for petrol and R1.30 for diesel at the start of July. If this trend continues, consumer inflation could rise toward 5% by year end, well above the current 3.6% forecast. This in turn might limit the Reserve Bank's ability to lower interest rates, even though the economy could use a boost.

Despite these headwinds, some positive signs remain. Real take-home pay is still expected to grow this year, which should help support consumer demand. And while salary growth is slowing slightly, it is still outpacing inflation. According to the Remchannel Salary and Wage Movement Survey from April 2025, salaries are projected to rise by an average of 5.82% this year, down slightly from 6.09% in 2024. This suggests employers are being more cautious, balancing pay increases with tighter budgets.

Employment, however, is showing worrying signs.

The most recent data from Statistics South Africa revealed that the formal non-agricultural sector lost 74,000 jobs in the first quarter of 2025. Overall employment dropped from 10.65 million in December 2024 to 10.58 million by March 2025. Over the year from March 2024 to March 2025, 95,000 formal sector jobs were lost. When including informal and household employment, the broader Labour Force Survey shows a more concerning picture: 291,000 job losses in Q1 alone, pushing the unemployment rate to 32.9%.

In short, while South Africans are seeing some gains in real income thanks to low inflation, the broader economic picture remains fragile. Rising fuel prices, weak job growth, and a tough global environment mean both workers and businesses are likely to stay cautious in the months ahead.
