Tech News NASA Faces Hurdles in Planetary Defence Efforts

June 25, 2025 ● Tebuho Zongwana

NASA is encountering serious challenges in carrying out its planetary defense strategy, raising concerns about Earth's ability to respond to asteroid or comet threats. The agency's mission to protect the planet from near-Earth objects (NEOs) has been slowed by technical delays, limited funding, and competing priorities.

A key setback involves the NEO Surveyor, an infrared space telescope intended to detect hazardous asteroids. Originally planned for launch in 2026, it has been pushed to 2028 due to budget constraints. Scientists warn that each year of delay reduces the chances of early detection, which is essential for any deflection effort.

NASA's 2022 Double Asteroid Redirection Test (DART) proved that it's possible to alter an asteroid's path using a kinetic impact. However, this method only works if a threat is spotted well in advance. Currently, Earth relies mostly on ground-based telescopes, which have limited range and visibility.

Although Congress recently approved a slight funding increase, experts say it falls short of what's needed. With high costs tied to programs like the Artemis Moon missions and Mars Sample Return, planetary defense remains a lower priority in NASA's budget.

NASA Administrator Bill Nelson said the agency is balancing multiple goals, but emphasized the importance of planetary protection. Scientists continue to call for more international cooperation, with missions like the European Space Agency's Hera set to provide crucial follow-up data from DART.

Many experts agree that while there is no immediate danger, the window to improve Earth's defenses is narrowing. Without consistent investment and a stronger global effort, the world could be unprepared when the next threat appears.
